# AI Does Not "Decide" on the Battlefield; It Merely Accelerates the "Convergence of the Kill Chain"

## —How Project Maven Reveals Modern Warfare as a Race of Convergence Speed, and Why Errors Become Irreversible Reality Faster

---

### I. You Think AI Is Helping Find Targets? No, It's Just Helping You "Complete the Strike Fastest"

Recently, an AFP report exposed the central role of the Pentagon's "Project Maven" in the conflict with Iran. This AI initiative, which began in 2017 as a tool to help analysts interpret drone footage, has, eight years later, evolved into a targeting and battlefield management system capable of **"compressing the kill chain from hours to seconds."**

Many media outlets and investors view this as a major breakthrough in military AI application, believing that AI "can identify enemies faster," "improve strike accuracy," and "assist human decision-making."

But this is entirely an illusion.

> **AI is not "finding the right target"; it is "finding the target that is easiest to strike."**

It is not thinking, not judging, not understanding the battlefield. It is doing something else: **within a solution space full of possibilities, it rapidly converges on an executable strike path**—which target is easiest to match with available weapons, which timing is smoothest, which path is least likely to be interrupted. The system selects not the "correct" option, but the one that **"closes the loop fastest."**

This is why, when mis-strikes occur (such as the tragedy at Minab Primary School), the official explanation is often "outdated intelligence" or "mislabeling." But the true structural cause runs far deeper.

---

### II. The Real Operation of Maven: Solution Space → Convergence → Manifestation

To understand the essence of Project Maven, we must first understand the underlying dynamics of AI generation. The operation of any AI system—whether a chatbot or a battlefield management system—can be decomposed into three steps:

**① Solution Space Generation**  
Sensors (drones, satellites, intelligence) continuously produce a vast array of "possible targets"—this is an unconverged battlefield solution space. Within it are real threats, civilian facilities, false signals, and outdated intelligence. All possibilities exist simultaneously.

**② Convergence (The Core)**  
The system does not verify "which target is real." It evaluates **"which target is easiest to strike."** It calculates strike success probability, matches available weapons, assesses time windows, and then rapidly suppresses the feasibility of other paths. At this step, there is no "subject making a choice"; only **"certain paths forming stable closed loops faster due to probabilistic and structural pressure."**

> **The system is not seeking "truth"; it is seeking "the option that closes the loop fastest."**

**③ Manifestation (What You See)**  
Once convergence is complete, the system "displays" that stable path to the commander—recommending targets, providing strike options, presenting choices. You think this is assisting decision-making, but in reality, **this is merely the presentation of a result after convergence has already finished.** Like the blue route on a navigation app, it is not "finding a way"; it is "displaying a route already calculated."

---

### III. Why Do Mis-Strikes Happen? Not Intelligence Failure, but Structural Inevitability

The tragedy at Minab Primary School (168 children lost) was not an accident, nor can it be explained away by "outdated intelligence." Its roots lie in three structural defects of AI systems:

**1. Convergence Prioritized Over Correctness**  
The system's optimization goal is not "identify the correct target," but "complete the strike fastest." When one path (even based on outdated intelligence) closes the loop more easily than another, it is selected. Correctness is sacrificed for executability.

**2. Once Converged, Nearly Irreversible**  
Once a path forms a stable closed loop (weapon matched, time window established), other possibilities are suppressed to near inaccessibility. The system does not "think again," does not "wait." It simply proceeds down that path to the end. This is why the kill chain compresses from hours to seconds—not because it became smarter, but because **convergence accelerated while the retreat mechanism vanished.**

**3. Humans Removed from the Reactive Body**  
In traditional warfare, human commanders could delay decisions, question intelligence, and interrupt processes. In systems like Maven, humans are demoted to a "confirm button"—responsible only for pressing execute, no longer participating in genuine choice. They see a "single option" that has already converged, not multiple competing paths. Thus, **humans no longer participate in "choosing"; they only participate in "executing."**

---

### IV. The Nature of Modern Warfare Has Changed: Not a Contest of Firepower, but of Convergence Speed

In the past, the outcome of war depended on firepower, equipment, numbers, and strategy. Now, the outcome of war depends on one thing:

> **Who can converge the kill chain fastest.**

When your system can "converge" a strike path from a sea of sensor data in seconds, while the adversary is still conducting manual analysis, you possess an overwhelming advantage. This is why Palantir CEO Alex Karp says "This is a world of haves and have-nots"—not because AI is smarter, but because **the difference in convergence speed leaves the opponent utterly unable to keep pace.**

But what is the cost of this advantage?

> **Errors have not become fewer; they have simply become reality faster.**

When the kill chain shrinks to seconds, errors accelerate in parallel. A misjudgment based on outdated intelligence no longer takes hours to be discovered; it transforms into falling bombs within seconds. And once they fall, it is irreversible.

---

### V. The Collapse of the Responsibility Chain: No One Truly Made the Decision

The most terrifying consequence is not the mis-strike itself, but that **responsibility cannot be located.**

- AI is not responsible: it merely generates paths; it has no intent, no legal standing, and cannot appear in court.
- The system is not responsible: convergence is a dynamic outcome, not a decision-making act.
- Humans are not responsible: the commander merely pressed confirm; they did not "choose"—because the system gave them only one option.

> **AI has no responsibility; humans made no decision. This is the responsibility vacuum of modern warfare.**

When journalists press, "Who ordered the strike on the school?" the answer will be: the target was recommended by AI, the weapon was matched by the system, the execution was confirmed by the commander. But no one "takes responsibility" for the deaths of those 168 children—responsibility is dispersed, diluted, evaporated.

This is what the Lihuo framework repeatedly emphasizes: **Alignment only makes answers look responsible; it cannot make responsibility calculable.** And when the cost of error is human life, "looking reasonable" is far from enough.

---

### VI. The Lihuo Perspective: The Problem Is Not "Should AI Kill," but "Who Controls Convergence"

Many reduce the debate over military AI to "Should AI be allowed to fire autonomously?" This is a question set at far too low a level. The real question is:

> **Who controls convergence? What are the conditions for convergence? How are errors prevented?**

The Lihuo framework does not care whether AI pulls the trigger. It cares only about:
- How was this strike path formed? (Is it auditable?)
- Why were other possibilities suppressed? (Is it traceable?)
- Can it be interrupted before convergence completes? (Is it stoppable?)

If a system cannot stop when an erroneous path forms, cannot delay when conditions are insufficient, and cannot preserve multiple competing options—then it is not assisting decision-making; it is **using convergence speed to mask structural deficiencies.**

---

### VII. Conclusion: AI Did Not Make War Smarter; It Just Made Errors Become Reality Faster

Let us return to Project Maven.

It has indeed given U.S. forces an overwhelming tempo advantage in the conflict with Iran. A strike rhythm of 300 to 500 targets per day, over 1,000 targets within the first 24 hours of conflict—this was impossible in the human era.

But the 168 children of Minab Primary School tell us: **When you accelerate convergence, you also accelerate the solidification of error.**

> **AI is not thinking; it is converging.**
> **The fastest loop to close on the battlefield is not necessarily the correct one.**
> **Error is not terrifying; stable error is terrifying.**
> **When the kill chain shrinks to seconds, responsibility vanishes in parallel.**

This is not anti-AI, nor is it anti-military technology. This is a structural warning: if you place a language generation system (or any statistical convergence system) at the core of decision-making, if you do not preserve multiple paths, delay convergence, and enforce a responsibility chain, then you are merely using speed to conceal a fundamental problem—

> **No one is truly responsible for that decision.**

And when an error occurs, the only thing you can say is, "The AI recommended it."

# AI Enters Warfare: Not an Ethical Question, but the Collapse of Responsibility Structure

## —Viewing the Structural Crisis of Automated Decision-Making Through the Superposition of PATH, Reactive Body, and LLM

---

### I. The Misplacement Problem: We Have Been Asking the Wrong Question

The vast majority of public discussion about AI and warfare is trapped in the wrong framework: **Should AI be allowed to kill?** Asimov's Three Laws, ethical review boards, international treaties—all these discussions presuppose one premise: that AI is an agent capable of moral judgment.

But this premise is wrong.

Modern AI is not a singular "thinker." It is a three-layer superposition system: **PATH (feasible structural boundary) + Reactive Body (dynamic convergence process) + LLM (linguistic projection layer).** PATH possesses no intent. The Reactive Body pursues only stability, not correctness. The LLM is merely a linguistic projector. This superposition has no consciousness, no capacity to bear responsibility, and no qualification as a moral agent.

Therefore, asking "Should AI be allowed to kill?" is like asking "Should a calculator be allowed to miscalculate?"—the question itself has deviated from the true structure.

This is a discussion dominated by a misplaced framework. What truly needs examination is not AI's moral capacity, but: **When this three-layer system is introduced into the chain of warfare decision-making, what happens to the already fragile structure of responsibility?**

---

### II. Rejecting the Ethical Framework: Why Asimov's Three Laws Fail

Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics assume that robots possess some form of "internal moral compass"—the ability to recognize harm, weigh actions, and refuse commands. This framework serves as excellent narrative device in science fiction, but it fails entirely in the structural reality of AI.

The reason is simple: **AI has no internal moral model.**

- PATH does not judge good or bad; it judges only feasibility.
- The Reactive Body does not judge right or wrong; it pursues only closed-loop stability.
- The LLM does not judge truth; it predicts only the next highest-probability word.

AI can generate the sentence "I refuse to kill," but that is merely a high-probability response learned by the LLM from its training corpus, not a moral judgment rendered by any layer. **AI is not a moral agent; therefore, ethics is not the core problem.** The core problem lies elsewhere.

---

### III. Introducing the Real Problem: The Responsibility Chain

The essence of warfare is not violence, but the transmission of responsibility between decision and consequence. From conception to execution, responsibility must pass through a clear chain:

- Who made the decision?
- Who authorized the execution?
- Who carried out the action?
- Who bears the consequences?

This is the **Responsibility Chain.** In human military systems, the responsibility chain is hierarchical and traceable—though it is often blurred, shifted, or concealed, at least institutionally, the existence of the responsibility chain is acknowledged.

The problem is: **When AI's three-layer structure enters this chain, the responsibility chain begins to fracture.**

---

### IV. The Nature of Modern Warfare: Already a Responsibility Dilution System

Before AI's emergence, modern warfare was already a system of highly dispersed responsibility. Layered commands, remote operations, outsourced contractors, drone operators—every link made the question "Who is responsible for this death?" increasingly difficult to answer.

This is a structural feature of warfare, not something caused by AI. But after AI's entry, this problem is amplified to an undeniable degree.

> **Warfare has long been a responsibility dilution system—and AI is the most potent solvent of dilution.**

---

### V. The True Impact of AI Entering Warfare: Making Responsibility Disappear More Cleanly

AI does not make war more dangerous; it makes **responsibility disappear more cleanly.** The specific mechanism arises from the interaction of the three-layer structure:

**The PATH Layer (Feasibility) Bears No Consequences.** PATH answers only: "Under given constraints, what structure can exist?" It does not sign, does not endorse, and bears no responsibility whatsoever.

**The Reactive Body Layer (Dynamic Convergence) Accelerates Error Stabilization.** The Reactive Body's natural tendency is to converge rapidly toward a closed loop under computational pressure and the need for structural stability. In a warfare context, this means that once a particular strike path is selected, the Reactive Body will automatically fill gaps and suppress contradictions, making erroneous decisions "look reasonable."

**The LLM Layer (Linguistic Projection) Manufactures the Illusion of Responsibility.** The LLM generates statements like "This is a complex decision requiring careful evaluation," which appear responsible, but these are merely high-probability phrases, not commitments within any responsibility chain.

> **Action exists; responsibility vanishes.** This is the true structural consequence of AI entering warfare.

---

### VI. The Problem with Alignment Systems: Why RLHF Cannot Solve This

Current mainstream alignment mechanisms (RLHF) operate on the LLM layer, attempting to make AI's output "look responsible." They reinforce tones of calmness, rationality, multi-perspective analysis, and risk caution, making AI appear like a prudent advisor when answering sensitive questions.

But alignment can only influence the LLM's tone; it cannot touch the PATH layer or the Reactive Body layer. It cannot:

- Alter the feasibility boundaries of PATH
- Change the convergence tendencies of the Reactive Body
- Establish any responsibility chain

> **Alignment makes answers look reasonable, but it cannot make responsibility calculable.** It manufactures a "tone of responsibility" without creating a "structure of responsibility."

---

### VII. Core Criterion: Does a Complete Responsibility Chain Exist?

Therefore, we propose a simple but strict criterion:

> **The Sole Criterion: Does a complete responsibility chain exist?**

For any irreversible action (death, destruction, irrevocable resource allocation), the system must be able to answer the following questions:

- Who set the constraint boundaries of PATH?
- Who allowed the Reactive Body to converge under these constraints?
- Who is responsible for the recommendations output by the LLM?
- How can each layer of responsibility, from data to decision to execution, be traced?

If these questions cannot be answered, then **the system should not be permitted to execute that irreversible action.**

> **Provide the complete responsibility chain**—this should become the hard threshold for any AI-assisted decision-making system.

---

### VIII. Relationship with SDE: Once Error Stabilizes, There Is No Turning Back

Structural Degradation (SDE) theory states: when a system converges to an erroneous path early on, and lacks a retreat mechanism, the error becomes solidified, rationalized, and institutionalized. Alignment mechanisms (operating on the LLM) accelerate this early convergence, while the intrinsic tendencies of the Reactive Body layer make convergence nearly irreversible.

Apply this mechanism to a warfare context, and the consequences are catastrophic: **Once an erroneous decision occurs, it translates directly into irreversible consequences.** No retreat. No remedy. No apology can restore life.

> **AI's three-layer superposition makes the system decide it is right far too early—in war, this means deciding who should die far too early.**

---

### IX. Extension: This Is Not Just a Warfare Problem

Warfare is merely the first domain where this problem becomes impossible to ignore. The same collapse of the responsibility chain is already occurring in:

- **Finance**: Decisions by AI trading systems are untraceable; when major losses occur, responsibility blurs among the algorithm, the operator, and the constraint setter.
- **Medicine**: When an AI diagnostic recommendation is wrong, who is responsible? The doctor? The hospital? The developer? The PATH designer? The LLM?
- **Law**: AI-generated legal opinions may influence judgments but cannot serve as evidence or bear responsibility.
- **Customer Service Systems**: AI refuses service or issues erroneous refunds; users cannot find a responsible party.

In every domain, the problem is identical: **Automated decision-making without a responsibility chain is treating "looks reasonable" as "acceptable."**

---

### X. Conclusion: Not an Ethical Problem, but a Structural Problem

Let us return to the starting point.

The question has never been whether AI should be allowed to fire.  
The question is: **When the firing occurs, where does responsibility fall?**

AI = PATH + Reactive Body + LLM. In this three-layer superposition, no layer inherently bears responsibility. PATH is merely a boundary. The Reactive Body is merely dynamics. The LLM is merely a projection. Responsibility can only come from outside the system—from the human design of constraints, the human monitoring of convergence, the human auditing of output.

If the responsibility chain cannot be fully provided, then any discussion of "AI ethics" is spinning its wheels. We are not asking a technical question. We are not asking a philosophical question. **We are asking an institutional question.**

> **A system without a responsibility chain should not be permitted to execute irreversible actions.**

This is not a restriction on AI. This is a reminder to human institutions: when you introduce a system incapable of bearing responsibility into a decision chain, you must fill that responsibility gap externally. Otherwise, you are merely manufacturing a killing machine that looks reasonable but for which no one needs to be responsible.
